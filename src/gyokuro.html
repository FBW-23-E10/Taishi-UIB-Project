Gyokuro is a type of Japanese tea. According to the "Labeling Standards for Green Teas" of the Japan Tea Industry Association, gyokuro is defined as "tea leaves plucked from tea gardens that have been almost completely blocked from sunlight by covering the first tea shoots with a reed rack or other covering material such as straw or cold gauze for about 20 days from the time they begin to grow. Generally, theanine, which is responsible for the flavor of tea, is produced in the roots and stored in the leaves via the trunk. When theanine is exposed to sunlight, it changes into catechins, which cause astringency. In other words, tea leaves, the raw material for gyokuro, are covered with a sun-shade prior to harvest (at least two weeks), which increases the amount of amino acids such as theanine and conversely decreases the amount of catechins (so-called tannins).
